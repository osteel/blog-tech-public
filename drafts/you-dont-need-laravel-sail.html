The announcement caused a wave of excitement across the community, as a lot of people welcomed Laravel Sail as a way to finally get into Docker. It also left some confusion in its wake, which compelled me to writing this piece about what to expect from it and, maybe more importantly, what not to expect.

But to get there, we first need to take a look under the deck, starting with a high level definition of what Sail is.

What is Laravel Sail?

Sail is Laravel's latest official development environment, completing an already rich collection comprising Homestead and Valet on the official side, as well as non-official solutions such as Laragon, Laradock, Takeout or Vessel (according to Otwell, Sail is largely inspired by the latter).

Sail is based on Docker, a technology leveraging containers to essentially package up applications so they run quickly and easily anywhere.

The Laravel team seems to have big plans for Sail, as the documentation overhaul it came with places it at the center of the Laravel experience, suggesting it is aimed at replacing solutions like Homestead and Valet, which occupied that spot until now.

How is it different?

So what is Sail's value proposition compared to its predecessors?

As a refresher, Homestead is a Vagrant box loaded up with everything most Laravel applications will need, including PHP, MySQL and a web server (Nginx) for the essentials, but also other technologies like PostgreSQL, Redis, Memcached, or Node. Valet, on the other hand, is a lightweight environment for macOS focusing on performance, installing and configuring PHP on the local machine directly instead of relying on a virtual machine, and plugging with other services like DBngin or Takeout to manage databases, for instance.

On paper these solutions look quite different; but these environments as well as the aforementioned non-official solutions all have something in common: they aim at managing all of your Laravel projects. In other words, the same environment serves all your local applications, and tries to anticipate and cater for most Laravel applications' needs.

It's true that one could use a different Homestead box per project, but in practice, most people don't, as doing so is quite cumbersome.

Laravel Sail is different, in that the environment setup is versioned with the rest of the codebase. This is not an option, but the default behaviour. This is, in my opinion, the most exciting thing about Laravel Sail: with this approach, your application comes with its own development environment, which is just a few commands away, provided you've got Docker installed on your machine.

You can now build development environments tailored to each project's needs, embedded in the codebase. Anyone with Docker installed on their machine can now just pull your code, and run sail up to have the full environment running in seconds, no matter what their operating system is.

That is the true power of Docker for development, in my opinion.

Sail comes with a few interesting things out of the box and make it easy to build upon them, in a way which is much easier than what the other options make possible.

Let's take a look at these features, and at the way they're implemented through Sail.

As mentioned earlier, the new documentation now makes Sail the preferred way to get started with Laravel; with a single command (which will be different based on your operating system), the necessary Docker images will be pulled, and the Composer dependencies installed automatically.

You're then invited to run the following command from the project's root:

$ ./vendor/bin/sail up

This one will pull the Docker image for MySQL if it's not present on your system yet, and build the image whose container will run the actual Laravel application.

So what's wrong with Laravel Sail?

If you got this far, you're probably wondering what still could be wrong about Laravel Sail, now that we've seen all of these strategies to extend it. Let me break it to you right now: now that you know and understand all of these strategies, you simply don't need Laravel Sail anymore.

But before I elaborate on this, let's review the current limitations of the environment, even though I fully expect the Laravel team to address most of them sooner or later.

That's right ‚Äì if you understood all of the above, there's hardly any reason to artificially constrain yourself within the confines of Laravel.

That's right, you could basically write your own version version of it, without artificially restraining yourself to the borders of Laravel.

Based on php artisan serve, no easy way to add a proper HTTP server. An HTTP server opens the door to domain names, HTTPS, and exposing local containers to the Internet (that one's not true anymore). The Bash layer can't easily be extended (although maybe can write your own Bash file and import the sail file).

Extensions?

These limitations are currently hard to circumvent, and combined with the knowledge we've acquired so far, lead to the main point.

You're one step away from total freedom

If you want to do anything useful beyond displaying the welcome page, you will need to extend Sail. That means you will have to understand how to customise the docker-compose.yml file, and probably how to write your own Dockerfiles. You won't be able to easily add new Bash commands and will probably run Docker commands directly instead.

With all this being said and done, what would prevent you from writing your own docker-compose.yml file, or your own Bash shortcuts?

Nothing.

Once you get there, there's absolutely nothing preventing you from doing your own stuff, without the constraints brought about by Laravel Sail.

So why not just do it?

The implications go even further. Why just use Docker for Laravel? Sail is contained in the Laravel application, but what if your application is not a monolith, and Laravel is just a part of it? Maybe you've got a separate frontend, and Laravel is used as its API? If that's the case, you might want a development environment that will manage them both, running them together just like on a staging environment or in production. If you like your monorepos, your Docker configuration could be at the root of the project, and you could have your frontend and backend applications is separate subfolders under an src folder. That would essentially look like this:

Even if your project is strictly based on Laravel, this kind of structure is already a cleaner separation than having the development files mixed-up with the application's source code. Plus, if your application grows bigger and needs other components besides Laravel, you're already in a good position to support them.

You can even reuse Sail's Dockerfiles (there's currently a PHP 7.4 version and a PHP 8 one).

The point is, once you break that mental barrier, everything is possible. You don't need to live in the constraints of one particular framework anymore, and you regain full control of your development environment.

There is one aspect of using Docker for local development, however, which applies to both approach and should be carefully considered before taking the plunge.

The elephant in the room

It would be intellectually dishonest of me not to mention it at this stage

There is one major limitation which does not seem to be mentioned anywhere so far, however, which relates to performance. While it should be perfectly fine on Linux and acceptable on Windows as long as WSL 2 is used, on macOS you will most likely experience a rather slow environment. I won't go into too much detail here, but it's essentially because of the filesystem, which does not perform well for mounted local directories.

This is Sail's current approach as we've seen earlier, and based on the time it takes Laravel's welcome page to display on my Mac, this issue hasn't been addressed.

That doesn't change the point, however: if you are comfortable with performance as it is, there is still no reason to use Laravel Sail over your own custom implementation.

Conclusion

Don't get me wrong: Laravel Sail is a great step in the right direction when it comes to web development, and I am glad to see such an established actor push forward the adoption of Docker for local environments.

We love our frameworks because they provide guidelines to achieve desirable results in a way we know to be efficient and battle-tested, and it's only natural that they also provide the environment that will allow us to follow and apply those guidelines. But what Laravel Sail unintendedly shows us is that the latter doesn't have to be part of the mandate anymore.

Much like Truman's small sailboat helps him overcome his fear of the sea and eventually gets him to the exit of his gilded cage, Laravel Sail can show us both the natural limits of the framework and a way to break free of them.

But Sail is only the beginning of the journey. It is quite limited and somewhat limiting in its current form, and is unlikely to make people Docker experts all of a sudden. Docker's major strengths when it comes to development is its portability, the flexibility it offers, and the way it empowers developers to easily install and run new services.

Moreover, Docker naturally makes you think at the system level (your application as a part of a whole) instead of boxing you to the confines of the application. The bigger picture now appears to you, and you become a better developer for it.

If this sounds exciting but you're still unsure how to get started, I invite you to take a look at the series I published on the subject. It covers web servers, HTTPS, domain names, and many other things, using Laravel and VueJS as example applications. No prior knowledge of Docker is required. It doesn't have all of the answers, but will get you to a place where you can find your own.

You might currently be in a position where Laravel Sail is more than enough for your needs. Or you may think you're not yet ready to venture in the outside world on your own, and you'd rather stay in the safety of an environment carefully designed for you. Or you just can't be bothered. That's all fine. To be honest, I fully expect Laravel Sail to evolve in a way that will cater for more and more use cases, and you as a developer may even grow with it.

But it will always be limited by its monolithic nature, by this vision of Laravel as a one-stop shop solution. This might work for a while, but no application exists in a vacuum, and some day your Laravel application will be just one component of a system, and not the system itself. Eventually, your small sailboat will bump into the painted backdrop of this walled garden, and you will be faced with a choice.

What you do next is entirely up to you; just know that there's a whole world out there, waiting for you. You could choose to embrace it right now.

That's what it takes for him to eventually bump into the painted backdrop of his walled garden, without which he wouldn't have had access to that door.
